

What is Diabetes?

Description:
Diabetes is a long-term condition where the body cannot properly use or make enough insulin — a hormone that helps sugar (glucose) move from the blood into the body’s cells for energy.
When insulin doesn’t work well, sugar stays in the blood, leading to high blood sugar levels (hyperglycemia).

Purpose:
To help users understand the basic idea of diabetes in simple, friendly terms.

Keywords: diabetes, blood sugar, insulin, hyperglycemia

Details:

Insulin acts like a key that unlocks cells so glucose can enter and give energy.

When insulin is not working or missing, sugar builds up in the bloodstream.

High sugar levels for a long time can damage eyes, kidneys, nerves, heart, and blood vessels.

Example Q&A:

Q: What is diabetes in simple terms?
A: It means your body can’t use sugar properly. Sugar builds up in your blood instead of going into cells to give energy.

Types of Diabetes

Description:
There are several kinds of diabetes depending on how the problem with insulin happens.

Keywords: type 1, type 2, gestational, prediabetes

Details:

Type 1 Diabetes: The body stops making insulin. People need daily insulin injections.

Type 2 Diabetes: The body still makes insulin but cannot use it properly. It’s the most common form and often linked to weight gain, poor diet, or low physical activity.

Gestational Diabetes: Appears during pregnancy and usually goes away after birth, but increases the risk of future diabetes.

Prediabetes: Blood sugar is higher than normal but not high enough for diabetes. With healthy habits, it can often be reversed.

Example Q&A:

Q: What is the most common type of diabetes?
A: Type 2 diabetes. It often develops slowly and can sometimes be prevented by eating healthy and staying active.

Causes and Risk Factors

Description:
Diabetes can affect anyone, but certain factors increase the risk.

Keywords: obesity, lifestyle, family history, stress

Details:

Being overweight or not physically active.

Eating too much white rice, fried food, or sugary drinks.

Having a parent or sibling with diabetes.

High blood pressure, high cholesterol, or PCOS (in women).

Smoking, poor sleep, or long-term stress.

In Bangladesh, Type 2 diabetes is more common in adults with office jobs and limited activity.

Example Q&A:

Q: How can I reduce my risk of diabetes?
A: Maintain a healthy weight, walk regularly, eat more vegetables, and avoid sugary or oily foods.

Major Complications of Diabetes

Description:
If blood sugar stays high for many years, it can damage many parts of the body. These are called complications.

Keywords: eyes, kidney, nerves, heart, foot care

Details:

Heart Disease: High blood sugar damages blood vessels and increases risk of heart attack or stroke.

Kidney Damage (Nephropathy): Sugar harms kidney filters, causing protein loss and swelling.

Eye Damage (Retinopathy): Diabetes can damage small eye blood vessels and cause blurred vision or blindness.

Nerve Damage (Neuropathy): Leads to tingling, numbness, or burning pain in feet and hands.

Foot Problems: Poor blood flow and nerve damage can cause wounds that heal slowly.

Skin & Mouth Issues: Infections are more common if sugar is not controlled.

Example Q&A:

Q: Can diabetes cause blindness or kidney disease?
A: Yes, if sugar stays high for years. Regular checkups and good sugar control can prevent most of these problems.
