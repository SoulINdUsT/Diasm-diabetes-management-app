
Understanding Insulin and Glucose

Description:
Insulin is the main hormone that helps control blood sugar. In diabetes, the body doesn’t make enough insulin or cannot use it well.

Keywords: insulin, glucose, hormone, blood sugar

Details:

Insulin moves sugar from blood to cells for energy.

When it doesn’t work, sugar stays in the blood and rises.

Managing insulin keeps energy steady and prevents complications.

Example Q&A:

Q: Why is insulin important?
A: It helps your body use sugar properly. Without insulin, sugar stays in the blood and causes damage over time.

When Insulin Is Needed

Description:
Some people with diabetes need insulin injections to keep sugar levels safe.

Keywords: insulin therapy, Type 1, Type 2, dosage

Details:

Type 1 diabetics always need insulin.

Type 2 diabetics may need insulin if tablets don’t work or sugar remains high.

Pregnant women with diabetes may also need insulin.

Example Q&A:

Q: Will I need insulin forever?
A: It depends. Some people need it for life (Type 1), others may control sugar with healthy habits and oral medicines (Type 2).

Insulin Types and Usage

Description:
Insulin comes in several forms that act at different speeds.

Keywords: rapid, short-acting, long-acting, basal, bolus

Details:

Rapid or Short-Acting: Taken before meals to control rise in sugar.

Long-Acting: Works all day to maintain steady sugar levels.

Mixed: Combines both in one injection for convenience.

Doses depend on body weight, sugar level, and doctor’s advice.

Injection Technique

Description:
Proper injection helps insulin work better.

Keywords: injection, rotation, technique

Details:

Use clean areas like stomach, thigh, or upper arm.

Rotate spots to avoid lumps under the skin.

Do not reuse needles.

Keep insulin cool but not frozen.

Example Q&A:

Q: Where should I inject insulin?
A: Usually in the belly or thigh. Change spots each time to prevent skin thickening.

Monitoring Blood Sugar

Description:
Checking sugar regularly helps prevent both low and high levels.

Keywords: self-monitoring, glucometer, HbA1c

Details:

Fasting sugar goal: below 7.0 mmol/L.

2-hour post-meal goal: below 10 mmol/L.

HbA1c target: below 7% (check every 3–6 months).

Record results in your app or diary.

Example Q&A:

Q: How often should I check my sugar?
A: Check fasting and 2 hours after meals a few times a week, or as your doctor advises.

Managing Emergencies

Description:
Sudden sugar changes need quick action.

Keywords: hypoglycemia, hyperglycemia, sick day rules

Details:

Low Sugar (Hypoglycemia): Shaking, sweating, or dizziness. Eat or drink something sweet immediately.

High Sugar: May cause thirst, tiredness, or blurred vision. Drink water and check medicine.

During Illness: Keep testing every 4 hours. Don’t skip insulin or medicines.
